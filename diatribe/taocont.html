Whatever we think becomes what we experience. It is just t a matter of time. We are continuously caught in the push and pull of sensory experience. Do you not notice this is true? You have to pay attention once in awhile -  it is you. What are your likes and dislikes.?... No one has the same; no matter what we may be told....

And if you grew up, as I nearly did, you studied historical accounts describing humanity as a bunch of murderer- rapist- thieves banding together waving flags, wearing this or that funny outfit, slaughtering someone on the other side. If you stay at home a lot and watch the local news, and believe everything your are taught ih school -  you become a prisoner of your own guards: putting up security systems and iron bars on the windows and purchase a gun with which you or your husband or children can injure each other and cause severe rifts in your household. 

But is this really the case? Or is it more like when we go to the cinema to watch some action packed film with lots of explosives and brash sex and people with their heads hacked off. How often do you see stuff like this when you're out shoppping, for example? Rarely, I suggest. And if you do, youshould  seriously consider moving to a different neighborhood. Seiously,  we are all  pretty much the same human being.  We go to such cinemas precisely because these things are not experienced daily in our city streets. Just as we watch the news to learn about things we wouldn't know otherwise. And so it is with history. If it were reported to young school children that most of the time, their ancestors were really more preoccupied with growing food, providing shelter, paying bills, etc., these poor school children would never pay attention to anything we taught them.  Despite whatever  grading curve and punishments we may offer. School children would rather pretend that there were superheroes and supervillians, despite the fact that they have to deal -  chiefly and daily  - with one another.

This is not to say that the world has been a bed of roses, either. Each of us have a lot of bad habits. People have a habit of creating confict with each other. Good news: This is not  genetic. This is not something we can not now or forever outgrow. We tend to view things like ths  because we tend to view ourselves as individuals engaged in some kind of conquest against other individuals who are engaged in the same conquest against us. What bullshit. We need each other. We rely on each other daily. Not merely the ones we meet in our day to day interaction, but everything we pick up -- every tool we use; every material -- we very deeply and entirely rely upon others, living or dead. None of us - no matter what our strengths and efforts can make it on our own. And ever much so, our pleasures and satisfactions as human beings. Check it out - that's all I ask - how happy you would be if it is was up to you alone. IF, under your total and global control, would  you - in fact- be  happy? Our bank is an accumulation of works over many generations and cultures, not oneself alone. Our technological arts include language by which we speak to one another, as well as gestures, altars and arts. We do not merely exchange money, we exchange values; What is the basis of these values? Our value is based largely on the basis of self. Think about this - what is more valuable TO YOU than yourself? And yet, as a basis of value - is this in itself REALLY valuable? Check it out .  Would  you be happy on  your own?

We don't know. There is not such an example. It is only as a result of much misinformation about ourselves that we have devised a great scheme of interactions and have incorporated them into our daily lives: the masks we portray to each other in conversations, the manner in which we drive our cars, the rhetoric of our politicians, and so on. We pay homage to the fiction of the competitive will in each of us, ambitions and an individual person or soul who manifests great achievement in strife with everything else.

I propose that the reality that makes all this possible, that provides each of us with a continuation of our lives and standards of living, and maintains our humanity in all of its splendor, is not these individuals out there slugging their way through a marketplace. Rather, it is our commonality and cooperation. This is my thesis. And to support such a way of looking at things, I will present my own historical accounts, pyschology and personal reflection.
